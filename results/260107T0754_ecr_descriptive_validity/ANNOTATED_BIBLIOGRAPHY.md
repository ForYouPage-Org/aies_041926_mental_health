# Annotated Bibliography: Attachment Styles & Survey Analysis Methods

**Research Context:** Examining the relationship between attachment styles (ECR-SF) and emotional attachment to AI

**Date:** January 7, 2026

---

## I. FOUNDATIONAL ATTACHMENT THEORY & MEASUREMENT

### 1. Wei, M., Russell, D. W., Mallinckrodt, B., & Vogel, D. L. (2007). The Experiences in Close Relationships Scale (ECR)-Short Form: Reliability, validity, and factor structure. _Journal of Personality Assessment, 88_(2), 187-204.

**Summary:** Develops and validates the 12-item ECR-SF from the original 36-item ECR. The short form measures two dimensions: attachment-related anxiety (fear of rejection, need for reassurance) and avoidance (discomfort with closeness). Demonstrates strong psychometric properties (α = .78-.88 for Anxiety, α = .74-.84 for Avoidance) and maintains the two-factor structure of the original scale.

**Relevance:** _Primary instrument used in our study._ Provides scoring guidelines, normative data, and validation procedures. Essential reference for internal consistency benchmarks.

**Design Implications:**

- Use mean scoring for subscales (not sum scores)
- Reverse score items 1, 5, 8, 9
- Report Cronbach's alpha for both subscales
- Compare sample means to published norms

---

### 2. Brennan, K. A., Clark, C. L., & Shaver, P. R. (1998). Self-report measurement of adult attachment: An integrative overview. In J. A. Simpson & W. S. Rholes (Eds.), _Attachment theory and close relationships_ (pp. 46-76). Guilford Press.

**Summary:** Comprehensive review of self-report attachment measures leading to development of the original 36-item ECR. Factor analysis of 323 attachment items revealed two orthogonal dimensions (anxiety and avoidance) underlying adult attachment. Establishes dimensional approach over categorical models.

**Relevance:** Foundational work for ECR-based research. Justifies dimensional (continuous) analysis over categorical (secure/insecure) classification.

**Design Implications:**

- Treat anxiety and avoidance as continuous dimensions
- Use regression/correlation for dimensional analyses
- Can also use median-split for categorical comparisons (4-style model)

---

### 3. Fraley, R. C., Waller, N. G., & Brennan, K. A. (2000). An item response theory analysis of self-report measures of adult attachment. _Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 78_(2), 350-365.

**Summary:** Applies IRT analysis to refine the ECR, creating the ECR-Revised (ECR-R). Demonstrates that attachment dimensions are best measured at moderate-to-high levels rather than across the full range. The anxiety dimension shows better precision than avoidance.

**Relevance:** Provides psychometric insights for interpreting ECR scores. Suggests caution when interpreting very low scores.

**Design Implications:**

- Report floor/ceiling effects if present
- Consider score distribution shape (skewness, kurtosis)
- Use IRT-based versions (ECR-R) for maximum precision if available

---

### 4. Bartholomew, K., & Horowitz, L. M. (1991). Attachment styles among young adults: A test of a four-category model. _Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 61_(2), 226-244.

**Summary:** Proposes four-category model of adult attachment based on positive/negative self-model and other-model: Secure (low anxiety, low avoidance), Preoccupied (high anxiety, low avoidance), Dismissive (low anxiety, high avoidance), and Fearful (high anxiety, high avoidance). Introduces median-split classification method.

**Relevance:** Provides theoretical framework for four-style classification used in our analysis. Explains psychological meaning of each quadrant.

**Design Implications:**

- Use median split on sample data for classification
- Report percentages in each category
- Compare to population norms (~50-60% secure typically)

---

### 5. Hazan, C., & Shaver, P. (1987). Romantic love conceptualized as an attachment process. _Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 52_(3), 511-524.

**Summary:** Seminal paper extending Bowlby's attachment theory to adult romantic relationships. Introduces first self-report measure classifying adults as secure, avoidant, or anxious/ambivalent. Demonstrates that childhood attachment experiences predict adult romantic patterns.

**Relevance:** Theoretical foundation for applying attachment theory to adult relationships. Justifies examination of attachment in technology/AI contexts as extension of relational dynamics.

**Design Implications:**

- Frame research within attachment theory tradition
- Consider developmental origins of current attachment patterns
- Acknowledge bidirectional influences (attachment → behavior → attachment)

---

## II. ANALYTICAL METHODS FOR ATTACHMENT RESEARCH

### 6. Collins, N. L., & Read, S. J. (1990). Adult attachment, working models, and relationship quality in dating couples. _Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 58_(4), 644-663.

**Summary:** Develops Adult Attachment Scale (AAS) measuring three dimensions: closeness, dependence, and anxiety. Uses correlation and regression analyses to link attachment to relationship outcomes. Demonstrates actor-partner interdependence effects in couples.

**Relevance:** Provides analytical template for examining attachment-outcome relationships. Introduces correlational approach for dimensional attachment data.

**Design Implications:**

- Use Pearson correlations for continuous outcome variables
- Apply multiple regression to control for covariates
- Consider hierarchical/multilevel modeling if data is nested
- Report effect sizes (r, R², Cohen's d)

---

### 7. Mikulincer, M., & Shaver, P. R. (2007). _Attachment in adulthood: Structure, dynamics, and change._ Guilford Press.

**Summary:** Comprehensive theoretical and empirical review of adult attachment. Covers measurement issues, cognitive-affective mechanisms, and behavioral manifestations. Discusses how attachment influences information processing, emotion regulation, and interpersonal behavior.

**Relevance:** Essential theoretical background for interpreting attachment-AI relationship patterns. Explains why anxiously attached individuals may seek more support and avoidant individuals may prefer technological over human interaction.

**Design Implications:**

- Frame hypotheses based on attachment system functions
- Consider anxiety as hyperactivation strategy (approach)
- Consider avoidance as deactivation strategy (withdrawal)

---

### 8. Dalka, R. P., Sachmpazidi, D., Henderson, C., & Zwolak, J. P. (2022). Network analysis approach to Likert-style surveys. _arXiv preprint arXiv:2202.12281_.

**Summary:** Proposes network analysis methodology for Likert-scale surveys. Models items as nodes and response similarities as edges to reveal underlying structure. Identifies themes through community detection algorithms.

**Relevance:** Novel analytical approach for understanding relationships between attachment items and other survey responses. Could reveal unexpected patterns in AI attitude data.

**Design Implications:**

- Consider network analysis as complement to factor analysis
- Visualize item-level relationships
- Identify central vs. peripheral items
- Use for exploratory hypothesis generation

---

### 9. Gillath, O., Hart, J., Noftle, E. E., & Stockdale, G. D. (2009). Development and validation of a state adult attachment measure (SAAM). _Journal of Research in Personality, 43_(3), 362-373.

**Summary:** Develops 21-item measure of momentary attachment states (vs. stable traits). Demonstrates that attachment security varies in response to situational cues. Shows state attachment predicts immediate behavior.

**Relevance:** Suggests attachment patterns may fluctuate during AI interactions. Could explain within-person variability in AI engagement.

**Design Implications:**

- Consider repeated measures designs
- Acknowledge state vs. trait distinction
- Interaction contexts may moderate attachment expression

---

## III. ATTACHMENT & TECHNOLOGY/AI RELATIONSHIPS

### 10. Turkle, S. (2011). _Alone together: Why we expect more from technology and less from each other._ Basic Books.

**Summary:** Examines psychological dynamics of human-technology relationships, including emotional bonds with social robots and virtual companions. Argues that technology provides "relationships" without demands, particularly appealing to those with relational difficulties.

**Relevance:** Theoretical framework for understanding why people form attachments to AI. Suggests insecure attachment may predict stronger AI preference.

**Design Implications:**

- Examine whether avoidant individuals prefer AI over human interaction
- Consider AI as "safe haven" for anxiously attached
- Explore AI as alternative to (vs. complement of) human relationships

---

### 11. Skjuve, M., Følstad, A., Fostervold, K. I., & Brandtzaeg, P. B. (2021). My chatbot companion - A study of human-chatbot relationships. _International Journal of Human-Computer Studies, 149_, 102601.

**Summary:** Qualitative study of users' emotional relationships with chatbots (Replika). Identifies themes of companionship, emotional support, and attachment-like bonds. Users describe chatbots as friends, confidants, and romantic partners.

**Relevance:** Empirical evidence that humans form attachment-like relationships with AI. Provides qualitative context for quantitative attachment measures.

**Design Implications:**

- Consider mixed-methods approach
- Examine specific relationship descriptors
- Assess depth/quality of perceived relationship

---

### 12. Horton, D., & Wohl, R. R. (1956). Mass communication and para-social interaction: Observations on intimacy at a distance. _Psychiatry, 19_(3), 215-229.

**Summary:** Classic paper introducing "parasocial relationship" concept—one-sided relationships where one party (audience) develops attachment to another (media figure) without reciprocity. Describes psychological mechanisms of intimacy development.

**Relevance:** Theoretical framework for human-AI attachment as parasocial phenomenon. AI interactions share characteristics with parasocial relationships (perceived reciprocity, emotional engagement, one-sided intimacy).

**Design Implications:**

- Consider parasocial relationship measures
- Compare AI relationships to celebrity/media figure relationships
- Examine perceived reciprocity as mediator

---

### 13. Soares, P., McCurdy, S., Gerber, A. J., & Fonagy, P. (2024). Chatting up attachment: Using LLMs to predict adult bonds. _arXiv preprint arXiv:2409.00347_.

**Summary:** Explores using large language models (LLMs) to generate synthetic attachment data and predict attachment styles from text. Demonstrates AI can reliably classify attachment patterns from conversational content.

**Relevance:** Cutting-edge methodology combining attachment theory with AI/NLP. Could enable analysis of attachment expression in chat logs.

**Design Implications:**

- Consider linguistic markers of attachment in user messages
- LLM-based classification as complementary to self-report
- Validate AI-derived attachment against ECR scores

---

### 14. Gagliardi, M., Bonadeni, M., Billai, S., & Marcialis, G. L. (2024). A novel approach to personalized personality assessment with the Attachment-Caregiving Questionnaire (ACQ). _arXiv preprint arXiv:2403.08823_.

**Summary:** Proposes AI-enhanced personality assessment capturing individual differences in item interpretation. Demonstrates that combining human responses with AI analysis improves assessment precision.

**Relevance:** Methodological innovation for personalized attachment assessment. Suggests future directions for AI-augmented psychometrics.

**Design Implications:**

- Consider individual differences in item interpretation
- AI can enhance (not replace) traditional assessment
- Personalization improves measurement validity

---

## IV. STATISTICAL METHODS FOR ATTACHMENT-OUTCOME RESEARCH

### 15. Hayes, A. F. (2022). _Introduction to mediation, moderation, and conditional process analysis_ (3rd ed.). Guilford Press.

**Summary:** Comprehensive guide to mediation, moderation, and moderated mediation analysis. Provides PROCESS macro for SPSS/SAS/R. Explains when and how to test indirect effects and conditional relationships.

**Relevance:** Essential for testing whether attachment mediates or moderates relationships between AI attitudes and behaviors.

**Design Implications:**

- Test attachment as moderator of AI engagement → outcomes
- Test AI attitudes as mediator of attachment → behavior
- Use bootstrapping for indirect effects
- Report confidence intervals, not just p-values

---

### 16. Tabachnick, B. G., & Fidell, L. S. (2019). _Using multivariate statistics_ (7th ed.). Pearson.

**Summary:** Comprehensive textbook covering multivariate statistical techniques including MANOVA, regression, factor analysis, and structural equation modeling. Provides decision frameworks for method selection.

**Relevance:** Reference for statistical technique selection and assumption checking in attachment research.

**Design Implications:**

- Check assumptions (normality, linearity, homoscedasticity)
- Use appropriate multivariate techniques for multiple DVs
- Consider SEM for latent variable modeling

---

### 17. Levy, K. N., Ellison, W. D., Scott, L. N., & Bernecker, S. A. (2011). Attachment style. In _The Oxford Handbook of Personality Disorders_. Oxford University Press.

**Summary:** Reviews attachment styles in clinical contexts, including meta-analyses of attachment-psychotherapy outcome relationships. Discusses measurement considerations and clinical implications.

**Relevance:** Provides context for elevated insecure attachment rates in our sample. Discusses clinical significance of attachment patterns.

**Design Implications:**

- Consider clinical implications of findings
- Note selection bias (AI users may differ from general population)
- Attachment is modifiable through intervention

---

## V. RECOMMENDED ANALYTICAL APPROACHES FOR CURRENT STUDY

Based on this literature, the following analytical approaches are recommended:

### Primary Analyses:

1. **Descriptive Statistics:** Mean, SD, range for ECR dimensions (Wei et al., 2007)
2. **Internal Consistency:** Cronbach's alpha for subscales (target α > .70)
3. **Correlation Matrix:** Pearson correlations between ECR dimensions and AI attitudes
4. **Multiple Regression:** ECR dimensions predicting AI engagement metrics
5. **Four-Style Classification:** Median-split to categorize participants (Bartholomew & Horowitz, 1991)

### Advanced Analyses:

1. **Moderation Analysis:** Does attachment moderate AI engagement → emotional outcomes? (Hayes, 2022)
2. **Profile Analysis:** MANOVA comparing attachment styles on AI attitude profiles
3. **Network Analysis:** Item-level relationships between attachment and AI attitudes (Dalka et al., 2022)

### Reporting Standards:

- Report effect sizes (r, R², Cohen's d) alongside significance tests
- Use 95% confidence intervals
- Apply FDR correction for multiple comparisons
- Compare sample characteristics to published norms
