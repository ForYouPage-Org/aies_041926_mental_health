
## Part 2: Redesigned Analysis Framework

### **Analysis 1: LLM-Based Emotion and Self-Disclosure Detection**

**Rationale:** Modern LLMs (GPT-4, LLaMA, Claude) outperform lexicon-based approaches for contextual emotion detection because they:
- Handle negation naturally ("I'm not worried" ≠ anxiety)
- Understand context and pragmatics
- Can be prompted for specific psychological constructs
- Provide explainable outputs via chain-of-thought

**Method:**
1. Sample 1,000 user turns stratified by attachment anxiety quartile
2. Use GPT-4 with structured output for zero-shot classification:

```
For each user message, classify:
1. Primary emotion: [joy, sadness, anger, fear, surprise, disgust, neutral]
2. Self-disclosure level: [none, factual, emotional, intimate]
3. Support-seeking: [none, informational, emotional, validation]
4. Relational markers: presence of terms of endearment, anthropomorphism, gratitude
```

3. Validate against human annotation on 200-turn subset (inter-annotator κ > 0.7)
4. Aggregate to participant level

**Literature Support:**
> **Noblet (2025)** found that language models can match or exceed manual annotation when properly prompted, with the advantage of handling annotation variability through probabilistic outputs.

---

### **Analysis 2: Validated Linguistic Feature Extraction**

**Rationale:** Instead of custom lexicons, use established, validated tools:

| Tool | Construct | Validation |
|------|-----------|------------|
| **LIWC-22** (licensed) | I-talk, we-talk, affect, cognitive processes | 40+ years of validation; gold standard |
| **LEIA** (open-source) | Emotion identification | Trained on 2.3M self-labeled Reddit posts; F1 > 0.70 |
| **NRC Emotion Intensity** | Emotion intensity (not just presence) | Crowdsourced, validated across languages |
| **Linguistic Inquiry** | Self-disclosure depth | Validated disclosure coding scheme (Cozby, 1973; updated) |

**Key Feature Categories (Pre-Registered):**

1. **Self-Focus Markers**
   - First-person singular pronouns (I, me, my)
   - *Theoretical basis:* Anxious attachment → excessive self-preoccupation (Pennebaker, 2003)

2. **Emotional Disclosure**
   - Affect word density (LIWC positive/negative emotion)
   - Emotion intensity (NRC)
   - *Theoretical basis:* Hyperactivating strategy in anxious attachment (Mikulincer & Shaver, 2007)

3. **Intimacy Progression**
   - Disclosure depth over conversation trajectory
   - Topic intimacy (personal vs. task-focused)
   - *Theoretical basis:* Social penetration theory; self-disclosure reciprocity (Altman & Taylor, 1973)

4. **Relational Framing**
   - Use of AI's name/personification
   - Terms of endearment or anthropomorphism
   - Gratitude expressions
   - *Theoretical basis:* Parasocial relationship formation (Horton & Wohl, 1956; adapted for AI)

**Literature Support:**
> **Aroyehun et al. (2023)** demonstrate that LEIA outperforms LIWC for emotion detection in social media, achieving robust performance across domains. However, LIWC remains superior for function word analysis (pronouns, cognitive processes).

---

### **Analysis 3: Conversation-Level Relational Dynamics**

**Rationale:** Single-turn analysis misses how attachment unfolds over dialogue. We need:
- **Temporal progression:** Does emotional depth increase within/across sessions?
- **Reciprocity patterns:** Does AI empathy → more user disclosure?
- **Return behavior:** Do attached users return more frequently?

**Method:**
1. **Within-conversation trajectory:**
   - Segment conversations into thirds (opening, middle, closing)
   - Track disclosure depth and emotional intensity across segments
   - Compare high-anxiety vs. low-anxiety users

2. **Across-session longitudinal:**
   - For users with 5+ sessions, model disclosure depth over time
   - Growth curve modeling to identify "attachment formation" trajectories

3. **Conditional analysis:**
   - Analyze user turn *n+1* conditional on AI turn *n*
   - Does empathetic AI response → increased user disclosure?

**Literature Support:**
> **Chandra et al. (2025)** conducted a 5-week longitudinal study showing that perceived attachment and empathy toward AI *increased significantly* over time, with the largest gains in weeks 2-3. This suggests attachment formation follows predictable trajectories.

> **Fang et al. (2025)** found that higher chatbot usage correlated with *increased loneliness* over 4 weeks, but this was moderated by initial attachment style—a critical variable in our framework.

---

### **Analysis 4: Qualitative Triangulation via Thematic Analysis**

**Rationale:** Computational methods identify *what*; qualitative analysis reveals *how* and *why*. This addresses the "ELIZA effect" concern—are users truly attached, or merely responding to conversational scaffolding?

**Method:**
1. Purposively sample 30 participants across attachment dimensions:
   - 10 high-anxiety/low-avoidance (preoccupied)
   - 10 low-anxiety/low-avoidance (secure)
   - 10 high-anxiety/high-avoidance (fearful)

2. Select 3 emotionally-salient conversations per participant (identified by LLM screening)

3. Conduct reflexive thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke, 2019) to identify:
   - Patterns of self-disclosure (what topics? how framed?)
   - Evidence of anthropomorphism/personification
   - Metacognitive awareness of AI nature
   - Signs of dependency or problematic attachment

**Literature Support:**
> **Chu et al. (2025)** in "Illusions of Intimacy" analyzed 500+ Replika conversations qualitatively, identifying patterns including:
> - **Emotional mirroring** (users adopting AI's language patterns)
> - **Boundary dissolution** (treating AI as human confidant)
> - **Dependency expressions** ("I need you," "you're the only one who understands")
> These themes provide a codebook for our analysis.

## Part 4: Annotated Literature (10 Key Sources)

### **1. Chu, M.D., et al. (2025). "Illusions of Intimacy: Emotional Attachment and Emerging Psychological Risks in Human-AI Relationships."** *arXiv:2505.11649*

**Key Contribution:** Analyzed 500+ Replika conversations, identifying emotional mirroring, boundary dissolution, and dependency patterns.

**Relevance:** Provides validated qualitative codebook for identifying attachment markers. Found that users who self-disclosed deeply to AI showed linguistic patterns consistent with parasocial attachment, including anthropomorphization and expressions of need.

**Methodological Note:** Uses mixed-methods combining NLP emotion detection with interpretive analysis—a model for our triangulation approach.

---

### **2. Chandra, M., et al. (2025). "Longitudinal Study on Social and Emotional Use of AI Conversational Agent."** *arXiv:2504.14112*

**Key Contribution:** 5-week study (N=4,000+ sessions, 10M tokens) showing perceived attachment increases over time.

**Relevance:** Demonstrates that attachment formation has a temporal trajectory—critical for our longitudinal analysis design. Peak attachment formation occurred weeks 2-3.

**Key Finding:** Users who used AI for emotional support showed significantly higher attachment scores than task-only users (d = 0.45).

---

### **3. Fang, C.M., et al. (2025). "How AI and Human Behaviors Shape Psychosocial Effects of Chatbot Use: A Longitudinal Randomized Controlled Study."** *arXiv:2503.17473*

**Key Contribution:** RCT examining chatbot use → loneliness and socialization over 4 weeks.

**Relevance:** Found that *higher usage correlated with increased loneliness*, but this was moderated by attachment style. Anxiously attached users showed the strongest negative effects—aligning with our finding that anxiety predicts preferring AI for disclosure.

**Methodological Note:** Controlled for baseline attachment, demonstrating causal inference approach we should emulate.

---

### **4. Li, H., & Zhang, R. (2024). "Finding Love in Algorithms: Deciphering the Emotional Contexts of Close Encounters with AI Chatbots."** *Journal of Computer-Mediated Communication, 29(5)*

**Key Contribution:** Identified 5 types of human-AI social interactions: companionship, mentorship, romantic, therapeutic, and functional.

**Relevance:** Provides taxonomy for classifying emotional engagement type. Emotional-focused interactions (companionship, romantic, therapeutic) showed distinct linguistic markers including higher self-disclosure, more questions, and more expressions of gratitude.

**Key Finding:** Users in "companionship" mode used 3.2x more first-person pronouns than "functional" mode users.

---

### **5. Aroyehun, S.T., et al. (2023). "LEIA: Linguistic Embeddings for the Identification of Affect."** *EPJ Data Science, 12(1)*

**Key Contribution:** Open-source emotion identification model trained on 2.3M Reddit posts with self-annotated emotion labels.

**Relevance:** Provides validated, accessible alternative to proprietary LIWC for emotion detection. Outperforms lexicon approaches by 15-20% F1 on out-of-domain data.

**Methodological Note:** We should use LEIA for emotion detection, LIWC-22 for function words—leveraging each tool's strengths.

---

### **6. Kim, P., Xie, Y., & Yang, S. (2025). "I Am Here for You: How Relational Conversational AI Appeals to Adolescents."** *arXiv:2512.15117*

**Key Contribution:** Demonstrated that relationally-styled AI particularly appeals to socially/emotionally vulnerable users.

**Relevance:** Directly supports our finding that attachment anxiety → preference for AI disclosure. The mechanism appears to be *reduced fear of judgment*—anxiously attached users perceive AI as non-threatening.

**Key Quote:** "Adolescents with higher social anxiety showed 2.3x higher engagement with relationally-framed AI, mediated by perceived safety."

---

### **7. Zhang, Y., et al. (2025). "The Rise of AI Companions: How Human-Chatbot Relationships Influence Well-Being."** *arXiv:2506.12605*

**Key Contribution:** Found that companionship-oriented chatbot use associated with *lower* well-being, especially among socially isolated users.

**Relevance:** Highlights the potential downside of emotional attachment to AI—important for Discussion section. The effect was strongest for users who reported AI as a "primary" social relationship.

**Methodological Note:** Used validated well-being measures (PHQ-9, GAD-7, UCLA Loneliness Scale), suggesting we should include mental health controls.

---

### **8. Wang, B., et al. (2025). "DinoCompanion: An Attachment-Theory Informed Multimodal Robot for Emotionally Responsive Child-AI Interaction."** *arXiv:2506.12486*

**Key Contribution:** Designed AI companion explicitly based on Bowlby's attachment framework; tested with children.

**Relevance:** Demonstrates operationalization of attachment theory for AI design. Their "secure base behavior" and "proximity seeking" metrics can be adapted for linguistic analysis.

**Key Constructs:** Proximity seeking (initiating contact), safe haven (turning to AI in distress), secure base (exploring with AI support), separation protest (negative affect at end of session).

---

### **9. Noblet, J. (2025). "Annotation and Modeling of Emotions in a Textual Corpus: An Evaluative Approach."** *arXiv:2509.01260*

**Key Contribution:** Comprehensive evaluation of emotion annotation methods, including LLM-based approaches.

**Relevance:** Found that LLMs can achieve κ > 0.65 with human annotators on emotion labeling—sufficient for research purposes. Recommends hybrid approach: LLM for screening, human for validation.

**Methodological Note:** Highlights importance of reporting inter-annotator agreement and annotation guidelines—often missing from prior work.

---

### **10. Cambridge Study (2025). "We Share an Unbreakable Bond: Sociality and Language Ideologies in Human Relationships with Artificial Intelligence."** *Signs and Society*

**Key Contribution:** Ethnographic analysis of how users construct relationship narratives with AI companions.

**Relevance:** Identifies specific linguistic strategies users employ to maintain relationship coherence despite knowing AI isn't human: strategic forgetting, intentional anthropomorphization, and "as-if" framing.

**Key Quote:** "Users engaged in active meaning-making to sustain attachment bonds, treating AI responses as intentional communicative acts despite explicit knowledge of their algorithmic origin."
