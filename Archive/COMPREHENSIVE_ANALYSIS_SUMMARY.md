## 🧠 Psychological Interpretation

### Why Anxious Attachment Predicts AI Emotional Use

**Attachment Theory Explanation:**

1. **Hyperactivating Strategy**

   - Anxious individuals intensify proximity-seeking
   - ChatGPT = Always available, never rejecting
   - Fulfills attachment needs without abandonment risk

2. **Secure Base Function**

   - AI provides comfort and reassurance
   - Consistent, predictable responses
   - No interpersonal conflict

3. **Emotional Regulation**
   - AI validates feelings without judgment
   - Immediate availability reduces anxiety
   - Safe outlet for vulnerability

**Evidence Across Methods:**

- ✅ Say they prefer AI for sharing (self-report)
- ✅ Actually use vulnerable language (behavior)
- ✅ Direct link between anxiety and vulnerability (correlation)

---

### Why Avoidance Shows Limited Effects

**Deactivating Strategy Explanation:**

1. **Emotional Suppression**

   - Minimize attachment needs
   - Avoid emotional engagement (even with AI)
   - Intellectual/task focus

2. **Disclosure Without Intimacy**

   - AI allows sharing without relationship threat
   - Can disclose but maintain distance
   - No follow-up demands

3. **Selection vs. Expression**
   - Affects CHOICE to use AI (self-report shows this)
   - Does NOT affect HOW they express in chats
   - May use AI for tasks, not emotion

**Evidence:**

- ✅ Report easier sharing (self-report, r = .31)
- ❌ Don't show vulnerable expression (behavior, r = -.12, ns)
- **Interpretation:** Use AI to avoid humans, but still suppress emotion

## 📝 Abstract-Ready Summary

### For Publication:

> **Background:** Attachment theory predicts interpersonal relationship patterns, but its applicability to human-AI interaction remains unclear.
>
> **Methods:** We triangulated three data sources: ECR attachment dimensions (N=212), self-reported AI attitudes (15 items, 5-point scale), and actual ChatGPT conversation logs (N=105, 10 million messages spanning 3 months to 3 years). Behavioral metrics included emotional language frequency and usage patterns.
>
> **Results:** Attachment anxiety significantly predicted 9 of 15 self-reported AI attitudes (r = .162-.441, p < .05), with strongest effects for disclosure preference (r = .441, p < .001), emotional relief (r = .323, p < .001), and feeling understood (r = .307, p < .001). Critically, self-reported "easier to share" predicted actual vulnerability language in conversations (r = .353, p < .001, N=86), providing behavioral validation. Attachment anxiety directly predicted vulnerability expression in chats (r = .279, p = .004, N=105). Avoidance predicted only disclosure preference (self-report: r = .305, p < .001) but no behavioral measures. Multiple regression showed anxiety as dominant predictor (β = .268-.530), explaining up to 21% of variance in AI attitudes.
>
> **Conclusions:** Converging evidence across self-report and behavioral data demonstrates that anxiously attached individuals form genuine emotional relationships with AI, showing both attitudinal preferences and actual vulnerable self-disclosure. Results validate attachment theory's extension to AI interactions and suggest attachment-informed design considerations. Anxiously attached users may be particularly vulnerable to AI dependency, warranting clinical attention and ethical safeguards.
