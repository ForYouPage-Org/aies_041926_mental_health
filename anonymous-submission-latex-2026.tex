\begin{abstract}
General-purpose conversational agents (GPCAs) such as ChatGPT now mediate a substantial share of young adults' information-seeking, but it is unclear whether users' current mental-health symptoms shape \emph{how} they engage with these systems. We analyze a Prolific-recruited sample of young adults (ages 18--25; $N=158$ for self-report, $N=104$ for conversational corpus, $N_{\mathrm{msg}}=19{,}930$ user messages) who completed validated mental-health screeners (PHQ-2 depression, GAD-2 anxiety, and PROMIS-7 anxiety as a psychometric robustness measure) and exported their full ChatGPT conversation history. We ask whether \emph{depression} --- with anxiety carried alongside as a methodological comparator --- predicts (RQ1) self-reported ChatGPT experiences, (RQ2) psycholinguistic patterns in users' prompts, and (RQ3) whether a participant's everyday prompts are sufficiently informative that a simple text-based classifier can recover their PHQ-2 / GAD-2 status above chance. We operationalize symptoms at clinically meaningful thresholds (positive screen $\geq 3$; total-distress severity tiers per \citet{lowe2010phq4}) rather than as continuous scores, motivated by the floor compression of two-item subscales documented in young-adult samples \citep{mavrogiorgou2025collabra}. Three findings: (i) Participants screening positive for depression report substantially elevated emotional engagement with ChatGPT (Cohen's $d=0.67$, $p_{\mathrm{FDR}}<.001$) and elevated behavioral change ($d=0.41$), self-efficacy ($d=0.37$), and trust ($d=0.34$, marginal); the parallel anxiety-positive contrast is narrower and does not replicate when anxiety is measured with PROMIS-7. (ii) Total-distress severity (4-tier PHQ-4) is associated with a coherent psycholinguistic footprint in users' prompts: 6 of 7 \emph{a priori} LIWC-22 features --- we-words, I-words, future-focus, affiliation, negative-emotion, total-social --- show FDR-corrected dose-response patterns. The footprint diverges from the canonical depression-language pattern (Tackman et al.\ 2019; Guntuku et al.\ 2022) in a distinctively interpersonal direction, suggesting that prompts to a GPCA constitute a novel linguistic context. (iii) A LIWC-features classifier recovers depression-screen status above chance (AUC = 0.589 [0.468, 0.705]) and anxiety-screen status (AUC = 0.637 [0.527, 0.741]), establishing that an implicit-disclosure channel exists; a literature-grounded per-item PHQ-2 / GAD-2 anchored prompting cascade \citep{galatzer2025llamadrs} run on a local 8B-parameter LLM (Qwen3) is essentially at chance (AUC = 0.533 / 0.513) -- the gap between dictionary-detectable signal and small-LLM-extractable signal indicates the easier privacy threat is not naive on-device LLMs but parties with frontier models, larger message budgets, or aggregate corpus access. We frame these results as evidence that current depressive symptomatology is implicitly disclosed through the texture of ordinary GPCA interaction and discuss implications for privacy, design, and the boundary between general-purpose and clinically-relevant AI.
\end{abstract}

\section {Introduction}

% General-purpose conversational agents (GPCAs) are now routine in young adults' information environment: ChatGPT has over 700 million weekly active users, and nearly six in ten U.S.\ adults under 30 report regular use. Unlike traditional search engines, ChatGPT's anthropomorphic, multi-turn interface invites conversation rather than querying, and a growing body of work shows that users bring emotional and relational patterns to these interactions even in ostensibly transactional contexts \citep{brandtzaeg2017chatbots,chatterji2025chatgptuse}. Recent evidence has linked adult \emph{attachment style} to how users experience ChatGPT: attachment anxiety predicts heightened emotional engagement, trust, and uptake of AI-suggested behaviors, while attachment avoidance predicts the inverse pattern for trust and self-efficacy, with detectable linguistic correlates in users' prompts \citep{anon2026attachment}.

If the concern motivating the attachment literature is that vulnerable users disproportionately seek out, trust, and confide in GPCAs, then an equally urgent empirical question is whether \emph{state-level} mental-health symptoms --- the anxiety and depression that fluctuate with life circumstances, and that primary care routinely screens with instruments like the PHQ-4 \citep{kroenke2009phq4} --- show the same pattern. Anxiety and depression are substantially more prevalent than clinically elevated attachment insecurity: roughly 20--30\% of U.S.\ young adults meet threshold symptom criteria at any given time \citep{nimh2023yamh}, and this prevalence rose sharply during and after the COVID-19 period \citep{czeisler2020mh}. Any vulnerability mechanism that activates through conversational AI therefore has substantially larger population exposure when it operates through depression or anxiety symptoms than through trait attachment alone.

Much of this work treats mental health as the dependent variable: does AI use cause changes in symptoms? The question we ask is the converse: do current anxiety and depression symptoms shape \emph{how} users experience and engage with a GPCA that is not marketed as a mental-health tool? If the answer is yes --- and particularly if symptoms leave a linguistic trace in users' prompts in ways users would not notice themselves disclosing --- this has direct implications for privacy, design, and regulation of the commercial systems that now mediate a substantial share of adults' information-seeking \citep{euact2026,gdpr}.

%Our results point to three findings. First, \emph{depression, not anxiety}, is the dominant state-level predictor of perceived experience with ChatGPT: PHQ-4 depression is the only FDR-significant bivariate predictor of the Emotional Engagement composite, and its partial beta in the controlled model remains substantial. Second, PHQ-4 depression is associated with a coherent psycholinguistic pattern in users' ChatGPT prompts --- elevated affect, future focus, we- and they-pronouns, affiliation, and social references --- that survives FDR correction at the bivariate level. PHQ-4 anxiety shows no FDR-significant linguistic associations. Third, the convergent structure of attachment-anxiety and state-depression effects (both predict engagement and leave a linguistic footprint) suggests that the privacy risk raised in prior work --- users implicitly disclosing psychological vulnerability through how they talk, not only what they say --- extends to common clinical populations, not only to those with insecure trait attachment. Where the state-depression footprint differs from the trait-anxiety footprint is its distinctly social quality (affiliation, we-words, they-words), consistent with depression's well-documented links to rumination about relationships and social withdrawal.

%We frame these findings as hypothesis-generating. Our sample is the same Prolific-recruited young-adult cohort used in the attachment study; it is not population-representative, and mental-health symptom rates in it are unusually high (49.5\% anxiety-positive, 38.1\% depression-positive on PHQ-4 screening thresholds). We report effect sizes, 95\% confidence intervals, and FDR-corrected $p$-values throughout, and we clearly flag which associations survive correction. Where we make design or policy claims, we align with prior calls to expand privacy frameworks beyond explicit PII to cover \emph{implicit psychological signatures} conveyed through conversational style \citep{anon2026attachment}.

ChatGPT, one of the most popular general-purpose conversational agents, has become an essential part of young people's everyday lives. By 2025, 34\% of U.S. adults had used ChatGPT, with the highest adoption rate among adults under 30 at 58\%; among U.S. teens, 26\% reported using it for schoolwork in 2024, twice the prior year's share~\citep{sidoti2025adults,sidoti2025teens}. This rapid uptake coincides with elevated mental-health vulnerability among young people: NIMH estimates that young adults aged 18--25 have the highest prevalence of any mental illness among U.S. adults, and CDC/NCHS data document particularly high rates of depression among adolescents aged 12--19 from August 2021 to August 2023~\citep{nimhMentalIllness,brody2025depression}. Understanding how mental-health symptoms shape young people's experiences with everyday conversational AI has therefore become an urgent question.

Growing research has examined the mental-health implications of conversational AI. Studies identify potential benefits such as emotional validation, social rehearsal, and private, immediate support, while also documenting meaningful risks, including over-reliance, withdrawal, and language indicative of loneliness or suicidal ideation~\citep{yuan2026mentalhealth,choi2025private}. Research on teen overreliance further documents patterns of attachment, mood dysregulation, sleep loss, academic decline, and strained offline relationships~\citep{namvarpour2026teenoverreliance}. Clinically, current LLMs remain unreliable substitutes for mental-health care: they can express stigma toward people with mental-health conditions, reinforce delusional thinking, and respond inappropriately in critical therapeutic scenarios~\citep{moore2025stigma}. Conversational AI thus occupies an uncomfortable position: emotionally powerful yet clinically unsafe.

Yet the existing literature is predominantly concerned with a single causal direction in which conversational AI affects mental health. Whether users' current mental-health symptoms shape how they experience and interact with a general-purpose conversational agent is less understood. Users do not always approach ChatGPT as a mental-health tool. They may begin with ordinary tasks, while their anxiety or depressive symptoms quietly shape whether the interaction becomes emotionally meaningful and habit-forming.

This gap also carries privacy implications. Psycholinguistic research suggests that mental-health symptoms can appear in language, but these signals are shaped by context. A randomized crossover trial found that no linguistic features consistently predicted depression or anxiety across writing tasks, underscoring the need to study such markers within specific communicative settings~\citep{odea2025linguistic}. Everyday ChatGPT interaction is therefore an important site of inquiry. If symptom-related traces appear in ordinary prompts, they represent a novel pathway for implicit psychological disclosure within commercial conversational systems, without users' awareness or consent.

These gaps motivate us to examine whether current depressive and anxiety symptoms shape young adults' experience of, and linguistic engagement with, ChatGPT. We center the analysis on \emph{depression}, with anxiety carried throughout as a methodological comparator: prior work on the same dataset \citep{anon2026attachment} found that \emph{trait} attachment anxiety is the dominant interpersonal-vulnerability predictor of GPCA engagement, but it is unclear whether a corresponding \emph{state-level} pattern is anchored in current depressed mood, current worry, or both. We address three questions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1.} How, if at all, does clinically-elevated depression (PHQ-2 positive screen) relate to users' perceived experiences with ChatGPT? Does clinically-elevated anxiety (GAD-2 positive screen, with PROMIS-7 anxiety as a psychometric robustness measure) show the same pattern?

    \item \textbf{RQ2.} How, if at all, does the gradient of total psychological distress (PHQ-4 4-tier severity) shape psycholinguistic patterns in users' actual ChatGPT prompts, against an \emph{a priori} hypothesis-driven set of LIWC-22 features?

    \item \textbf{RQ3.} How detectable is a participant's depression / anxiety screening status from their everyday ChatGPT prompts alone? We compare a LIWC-features baseline against a literature-grounded per-item PHQ-2 / GAD-2 anchored prompting cascade run on a local 8B-parameter LLM \citep{galatzer2025llamadrs,sadeghi2025cognitive}, framed as a lower bound on the implicit-disclosure channel.
\end{itemize}

To answer these questions, we analyze survey and conversational data ($N_{\mathrm{msg}}=19{,}930$) from young-adult ChatGPT users ($N=158$ with complete self-report data, $N=104$ with usable chat logs). We measure current depressive and anxiety symptoms with the PHQ-4 (PHQ-2 depression and GAD-2 anxiety) and the PROMIS-7 anxiety short form. We operationalize all predictors at clinically meaningful thresholds (binary screen positive at $\geq 3$; 4-tier severity for total distress per \citet{lowe2010phq4}), motivated by the floor-compression literature on 2-item subscales in non-clinical young-adult samples \citep{mavrogiorgou2025collabra,khubchandani2024phq4}. We examine perceived ChatGPT experiences across emotional engagement, trust, self-efficacy, behavioral change, and dependency concern; analyze users' actual prompts with LIWC-22 psycholinguistic markers \citep{boyd2022liwc}; and probe the detectability of distress from those same prompts using both LIWC-features logistic regression and a per-symptom anchored LLM-prompting cascade.

This study makes three contributions. \emph{First}, it provides one of the first naturalistic-conversation-grounded characterizations of how current depressive symptoms shape young adults' engagement with a general-purpose, non-therapeutic conversational agent --- distinguished from extant work on companion-CA effects \citep{maples2024loneliness,laestadius2024replika} and from the trait-attachment pattern \citep{anon2026attachment} on the same sample. \emph{Second}, it shows that a coherent psycholinguistic footprint of depression is detectable in ordinary GPCA prompts, with a marker structure that diverges from the canonical depression-language pattern (Tackman et al.\ 2019; Guntuku et al.\ 2022), suggesting that prompts to a GPCA constitute a novel linguistic context. \emph{Third}, it operationalizes the implicit-disclosure concern as a concrete detectability claim, showing that a participant's PHQ-2 status is recoverable from their everyday prompts above chance with both a feature-based baseline and a literature-grounded LLM cascade --- supporting the view that current privacy frameworks for GPCAs, focused on explicit personally identifiable information, miss implicit psychological signatures embedded in conversational style.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Mental Health and Conversational AI}
A large body of work has examined AI chatbot use as a \emph{cause} of mental-health change: reduced loneliness and anxiety from sustained companion-CA use \citep{maples2024loneliness}, reduced depression symptoms from therapy-focused CAs \citep{dosovitsky2021depression}, but also increased loneliness among heavy users \citep{phang2025affective}, emotional dependence on companion platforms \citep{laestadius2024replika}, and safety failures in crisis-adjacent adolescent conversations \citep{clark2025limits}. Our question is the converse: does current symptom load shape how users approach and experience a non-therapeutic GPCA? This framing is operationally closer to the attachment work that directly motivated us \citep{anon2026attachment}, and closer to population-level questions about who is most exposed.

\subsection{The Trait--State Distinction}
Attachment style is a trait construct, stable over years and measured with the ECR-SF \citep{wei2007ecrsf,bartholomew1991styles}. Anxiety and depression as measured by the PHQ-4 are explicitly state-like, indexing symptoms over the past two weeks \citep{kroenke2009phq4}; the PROMIS short forms measure state over seven days \citep{pilkonis2011promis}. The two levels are correlated but distinct: insecurely attached individuals are at elevated risk for depression and anxiety, but current mood can fluctuate widely for any given trait profile. Because state symptoms are far more prevalent than clinically elevated trait attachment insecurity, they are the more population-relevant lens for thinking about GPCA-mediated vulnerability.

\subsection{Psycholinguistic Disclosure and Privacy}
LIWC-22 \citep{boyd2022liwc} and related psycholinguistic frameworks have documented robust associations between everyday word use and both trait and state psychological variables --- emotional states, personality, mental health, and life outcomes. \citet{anon2026attachment} used LIWC-22 to show that trait attachment anxiety leaves detectable traces in ChatGPT prompts (I-words, affect, future focus, inverse analytical thinking), and argued that current privacy frameworks, which focus on explicit PII, miss these implicit psychological signatures. Our analysis tests whether the same framework applies to state symptoms.

\section{Method}

We conducted a three-part, mixed-methods analysis combining a multi-instrument psychometric survey with computational psycholinguistic analysis and an LLM-based detection probe of the same participants' real ChatGPT conversation histories. Data were collected through the research platform Prolific between November 2024 and July 2025. Participants were compensated at \$10 per hour. All procedures were approved by our institutional review board. The dataset and self-report instruments are shared with the parent study \citep{anon2026attachment}; our predictor set, operationalization, and analyses are distinct.

\subsection{Participants and Recruitment}
We recruited 510 young adults (ages 18--25) who self-reported using ChatGPT at least 10 times in the past two weeks. For the present study we included only participants who completed both survey parts (one part on demographics and psychological measures, one on ChatGPT experiences), yielding $N_{\mathrm{participant}}=168$. Chat logs containing fewer than 10 conversations or not primarily in English were screened out, leaving 105 participants with usable ChatGPT transcripts. Our analytic samples are: $N=158$ for the self-report analyses (RQ1; complete PHQ-4, age, usage-frequency, and AI-experience data; gender missing for 38 participants, mean-imputed following the parent study); $N=104$ for the psycholinguistic analyses (RQ2; participants with both complete self-report and chat-log data); $N=105$ for the LLM detection probe (RQ3; same chat-log subset). PROMIS-7 anxiety was administered to a later wave of recruits ($N=109$ available, $N=47$ with both PROMIS and chat logs); we treat PROMIS comparisons as a robustness check on the smaller available sample.

\subsection{Materials}
We used three classes of instruments: established mental-health screeners as the primary predictors, the ChatGPT-experience instrument from the parent study as the self-report outcome, and a small set of demographic covariates. Table~\ref{tab:constructs} summarizes constructs, items, scale anchors, and reliabilities.

\paragraph{Mental-health screeners (primary predictors).} The four-item PHQ-4 \citep{kroenke2009phq4} comprises a two-item PHQ-2 depression subscale (``Little interest or pleasure in doing things''; ``Feeling down, depressed, or hopeless'') and a two-item GAD-2 anxiety subscale (``Feeling nervous, anxious, or on edge''; ``Not being able to stop or control worrying''). Each item is rated on a 4-point scale (0 = \emph{not at all}, 3 = \emph{nearly every day}); subscale totals range 0--6, with the standard cutoff $\geq 3$ indicating a positive clinical screen. The PHQ-4 total ranges 0--12 and is interpreted in four ordered severity tiers: \emph{none} (0--2), \emph{mild} (3--5), \emph{moderate} (6--8), \emph{severe} (9--12) \citep{lowe2010phq4}. Two-item Spearman--Brown reliabilities were $r_{SB}=.80$ for GAD-2 anxiety and $r_{SB}=.68$ for PHQ-2 depression; the combined four-item Cronbach's $\alpha=.83$. As a psychometric-robustness measure for the anxiety construct we administered the 7-item PROMIS Anxiety short form (T-score-anchored; raw range 7--35; ``elevated'' threshold raw $\geq 18$, corresponding to $T\geq 55$ per HealthMeasures conversion) \citep{pilkonis2011promis}.

\paragraph{ChatGPT experiences (outcomes).} We used the 11-item ChatGPT-experience instrument from the parent study \citep[Table~3 of][]{anon2026attachment}, scored as three composites and two single items: \emph{Emotional Engagement} (3 items, $\alpha=.88$), \emph{Self-Efficacy} (3 items, $\alpha=.71$), \emph{Behavioral Change} (3 items, $\alpha=.71$), \emph{Trust} (Q1), and \emph{Dependency Concern} (Q13). All items use a 5-point Likert scale (1 = \emph{strongly disagree} to 5 = \emph{strongly agree}).

\paragraph{Covariates.} We controlled for age (continuous), gender (binary, mean-imputed where missing per the parent study), and self-reported ChatGPT usage frequency (ordinal, 0 = \emph{hadn't used ChatGPT} to 4 = \emph{20+ times in past two weeks}). For RQ2 / RQ3 (chat-log analyses) we additionally controlled for $\log(1 + n_\mathrm{messages})$ to absorb message-volume confounds.

\subsection{Conversation Log Data Collection}
Participants who consented to the chat-log component were instructed to use OpenAI's standard data export to download their complete ChatGPT conversation history in JSON, which they then uploaded to a secure submission portal (after a guided in-survey anonymization step that strips names, emails, and addresses from any donated text). Exported data included all user prompts, AI responses, conversation timestamps, and conversation-thread identifiers. Conversation histories ranged from three months to three years of interactions; most AI responses were generated by GPT-4o (49.6\%) and GPT-4o-mini (12.8\%). For psycholinguistic and LLM-probe analyses we restricted to user-typed messages under 20 words to exclude pasted essays and code blocks (mean message length $19.4$ words across the full export; the under-20-word filter retains $N_{\mathrm{msg}}=19{,}930$ user messages from the analytic sample).

\subsection{Sample Characteristics}
Mean age was $21.66$ ($SD=2.23$); 49\% female of those with sex reported. PHQ-2 depression: $M=1.91$, $SD=1.57$; 38.1\% screen-positive ($\geq 3$). GAD-2 anxiety: $M=2.59$, $SD=1.79$; 49.5\% screen-positive. PHQ-4 total severity tiers: 29.7\% \emph{none} (0--2), 30.4\% \emph{mild} (3--5), 29.7\% \emph{moderate} (6--8), 10.1\% \emph{severe} (9--12). PROMIS-7 anxiety ($N=109$): $M=18.19$, $SD=6.27$; 57.8\% above the elevated threshold (raw $\geq 18$). Anxiety and depression subscales were highly correlated ($r=.65$), in the range expected given their shared distress variance \citep{kroenke2009phq4}; this correlation motivates our reliance on subscale-positive screens (rather than partial-regression betas alone) for the primary RQ1 contrasts. See Figure~\ref{fig:dist} for distributions.

\subsection{Operationalization Rationale and Departure from the Parent Study}
The parent attachment study \citep{anon2026attachment} treated continuous ECR-SF subscales as approximately Gaussian, following established practice in attachment research where the underlying construct is dimensional and the items number 6+ per subscale. We adopt a different operationalization for two reasons. First, the PHQ-2 / GAD-2 subscales each have only two items rated 0--3 (range 0--6), and recent psychometric work on the PHQ-4 in young-adult non-clinical samples documents subclinical floor compression that limits the validity of treating the subscale total as a continuous Gaussian \citep{mavrogiorgou2025collabra,khubchandani2024phq4}. Second, the dominant convention in 2020+ health and HCI research is to use either binary screen-positivity ($\geq 3$ per subscale) or the four-tier total-distress severity bands (Löwe et al.\ 2010), both of which are clinically interpretable in a way that a centered 0--6 score is not. We therefore lead our reporting with categorical operationalizations and report continuous Pearson correlations (in the parent-study style) as a supplementary comparison.

\subsection{Analytic Strategy}
\paragraph{RQ1 (self-report).} Per outcome (5 ChatGPT-experience composites/items), we computed: (a) a Welch independent-samples $t$-test contrasting positive vs.\ negative subscale screens with Cohen's $d$ and bootstrap (2{,}000 iters) 95\% CIs on $d$; (b) a one-way ANOVA across the four PHQ-4 total-distress tiers with $\eta^2$ and a linear-trend test; (c) an OLS multiple regression with the screen indicator as predictor, controlling for age, gender, ChatGPT usage frequency, and the \emph{other} PHQ subscale (to isolate depression-specific from anxiety-specific effects given the $r=.65$ overlap). We also report Mann--Whitney $U$ and Kruskal--Wallis $H$ as nonparametric robustness checks. PROMIS-anxiety analyses are run on the available $N=109$ subset using the raw $\geq 18$ cutoff. We apply Benjamini--Hochberg FDR correction within each predictor-by-outcome family of 5 \citep{benjamini1995fdr}.

\paragraph{RQ2 (psycholinguistic footprint).} We extracted 46 LIWC-22 features per participant by averaging across their user messages under 20 words, following the per-participant aggregation rule of \citet{anon2026attachment}. To anchor inferential claims in prior depression-language theory and to reduce the FDR-correction burden inherent in 46-test contrasts, we pre-registered a primary feature set of 7 \emph{a priori} markers organized into two theoretical clusters (Table~\ref{tab:rq2_hypotheses}). \emph{Cluster 1 -- Canonical depression markers.} Three features have well-established directional associations with depression in non-AI-mediated text: elevated first-person singular pronouns (I-words) interpreted as self-referential rumination \citep{tackman2019pronouns,edwards2017pronouns}, elevated negative-emotion words from sustained negative affectivity \citep{eichstaedt2018facebook}, and elevated future-focused language, which the recent psycholinguistic literature reinterprets as worry / rumination rather than optimism \citep{thorstad2023future}. We expect to recover all three in the depression-positive direction. \emph{Cluster 2 -- Interpersonal markers.} Four features index interpersonal language: we-words, they-pronouns, affiliation, and total social references. The canonical direction in non-AI text is downward for we-words and affiliation -- depressed users tend toward social withdrawal and less affiliative language, which is in fact the discriminator that distinguishes depression from comorbid loneliness \citep{guntuku2022loneliness,tackman2019pronouns}. Third-person plural references (they) and total social references are direction-ambiguous in the literature: rumination on others can elevate them while withdrawal can suppress them \citep{nolenhoeksema2008rumination}. Against this canonical baseline, we test the alternative hypothesis that prompts to a general-purpose conversational agent constitute a distinct linguistic context where depressed users disproportionately engage in interpersonal-conflict processing and social comparison; under this alternative, the four interpersonal markers should be elevated (not suppressed) with depression severity, inverting the canonical direction. Per feature, we ran the same three-stage analysis as RQ1 (positive-screen contrast with $d$ and CIs; 4-tier ANOVA with $\eta^2$ and trend; OLS with covariates including $\log(1+n_\mathrm{messages})$). FDR is applied within the 14-test \emph{primary} family (7 features $\times$ 2 predictors); a separate exploratory analysis over all 46 LIWC features applies FDR within the 92-test exploratory family and is reported in supplementary results.

\paragraph{RQ3 (detection probe).} We constructed three classifiers predicting the binary subscale positive screen (PHQ-2 depression $\geq 3$ and, separately, GAD-2 anxiety $\geq 3$) from each participant's chat content: (A) a $\ell_2$-regularized logistic regression on the 46 LIWC-22 features (\texttt{LogisticRegressionCV}, $C \in \{10^{-2},\dots,10^{2}\}$, class-balanced), (B) a literature-grounded per-item PHQ-2 / GAD-2 anchored prompting cascade (described below) using the local 8B-parameter Qwen3 model \citep{qwen3} via Ollama, scoring participants on each PHQ-2 / GAD-2 item separately and summing to a 0--6 prediction, and (C) a stacked LIWC + LLM-cascade combined model. Performance is reported as repeated 5-fold cross-validation (5 repeats, stratified) AUC with bootstrap 95\% CIs (2{,}000 iterations) and top-30\%-recall as a use-case-relevant ranking metric. The LLM cascade methodology is grounded in three lines of recent NLP work on LLM-based mental-health detection: per-item rubric-anchored prompting (LlaMADRS; \citealp{galatzer2025llamadrs}) which achieved ICC = .96 with clinicians on MADRS; symptom-by-symptom decomposition as the dominant winning paradigm at the eRisk shared task \citep{parapar2024erisk,parapar2025erisk}; and chain-of-thought for binary item rating with deterministic aggregation across items \citep{sadeghi2025cognitive}. We use a researcher / IRB framing and four anchored synthetic exemplars (one per scale point 0--3) per item to mitigate the safety-tuned-model refusal pattern documented in open-weight models for clinical-screening tasks \citep{xu2024mentalllm,yang2024mentallama}. Up to 60 randomly-sampled user messages per participant ($\geq 4$ words) are concatenated into the prompt context. Prompt template, anchored exemplars, and per-item parsing are documented in supplementary materials and the released analysis code.

\section{Results}

\subsection{RQ1: Depression, Not Anxiety, Broadly Predicts Self-Reported ChatGPT Experience}

Figure~\ref{fig:reg} and Table~\ref{tab:rq1_binary} report the primary RQ1 contrast: positive vs.\ negative subscale screen on each of the five ChatGPT-experience outcomes ($N=158$). Participants screening positive on PHQ-2 depression ($n_+=58$, $n_-=100$) reported substantially elevated experiences across four of five outcomes. The largest effect was on \emph{Emotional Engagement} ($M_+=3.51$, $M_-=2.75$; Cohen's $d=0.67$ [95\% CI 0.34, 1.00], $t=4.09$, $p<.001$, $p_{\mathrm{FDR}}<.001$), followed by \emph{Behavioral Change} ($d=0.41$, $p_{\mathrm{FDR}}=.031$), \emph{Self-Efficacy} ($d=0.37$, $p_{\mathrm{FDR}}=.031$), and \emph{Trust} ($d=0.34$, $p_{\mathrm{FDR}}=.054$, marginal). Only \emph{Dependency Concern} did not differ by depression screen ($d=-0.15$, $p=.36$). Mann--Whitney $U$ tests yielded the same conclusions. In the OLS model controlling for age, gender, ChatGPT usage frequency, and GAD-2 anxiety as a covariate, the depression-screen effect on Emotional Engagement remained robust ($p_{\mathrm{FDR}}=.001$); other effects attenuated to marginal after controlling for the highly correlated anxiety predictor.

\paragraph{Anxiety screen contrast is narrower and not psychometrically robust.} GAD-2 anxiety screen positivity ($n_+=77$) showed a single FDR-significant effect, on \emph{Emotional Engagement} ($d=0.47$, $p_{\mathrm{FDR}}=.019$); behavioral change was marginal ($d=0.31$, $p=.055$). Crucially, when anxiety was re-measured with the 7-item PROMIS short form on the smaller available sample ($N=109$, $n_{\mathrm{elevated}}=63$), the Emotional Engagement effect did not replicate: the PROMIS-elevated vs.\ -not-elevated contrast was null on all five outcomes (all $p_{\mathrm{FDR}}>.65$, all $|d|<.25$). The most parsimonious interpretation is that the GAD-2 anxiety effect on Emotional Engagement was carried by depression variance bleeding through the $r=.65$ subscale correlation rather than reflecting an anxiety-specific channel.

\paragraph{Total-distress severity confirms a monotonic Emotional Engagement gradient.} Across the four PHQ-4 total-distress severity tiers (\emph{none} $n=47$, \emph{mild} $n=48$, \emph{moderate} $n=47$, \emph{severe} $n=16$; Figure~\ref{fig:tier}), Emotional Engagement showed a near-monotonic pattern of group means ($M=2.65,\,3.03,\,3.24,\,3.50$; one-way ANOVA $F(3,154)=3.09$, $p=.029$, $\eta^2=.057$; linear trend $r=.24$, $p=.003$). \emph{Dependency Concern} showed a non-monotonic but FDR-robust pattern ($M=3.32,\,3.81,\,3.09,\,4.25$; $F=5.64$, $p_{\mathrm{FDR}}=.005$, $\eta^2=.099$): only severely-distressed participants explicitly reported worry about over-reliance, while moderately-distressed participants reported the lowest dependency concern.

\paragraph{Operationalization comparison.} The continuous PHQ-2 depression sum (the operationalization of the parent attachment study) yielded a single FDR-significant correlation, with Emotional Engagement ($r=.27$, $p_{\mathrm{FDR}}=.003$). Categorical (positive-screen) operationalization recovers three additional FDR-significant outcomes (Behavioral Change, Self-Efficacy, marginal Trust) at substantially larger effect sizes ($d$ approximately 1.5--2$\times$ the equivalent continuous correlation). This pattern is consistent with the threshold-like nature of clinical depression screening cutoffs and is the operationalization recommended for the PHQ-4 in young-adult non-clinical samples \citep{mavrogiorgou2025collabra}.

\paragraph{Cluster 1 (canonical markers): all three features confirmed in the predicted direction.} The three Cluster-1 features all showed monotonic-or-near-monotonic dose-response across PHQ-4 severity tiers in the canonical $\uparrow$ direction. Negative-emotion words rose from $M=0.16$ in the \emph{none} tier to $M=0.47$ in the \emph{severe} tier ($F(3,100)=2.99$, $p_{\mathrm{FDR}}=.047$, $\eta^2=.082$). Future-focused language rose from $M=0.36$ to $M=0.74$ ($F=3.25$, $p_{\mathrm{FDR}}=.044$, $\eta^2=.089$), consistent with the worry/rumination reinterpretation of future-orientation in depression \citep{thorstad2023future}. I-words rose from $M=2.00$ to $M=3.37$ at the moderate tier (with a slight regression at severe, $M=2.44$; $F=3.52$, $p_{\mathrm{FDR}}=.044$, $\eta^2=.096$), consistent with the small but reliable self-referential-rumination effect documented by \citet{tackman2019pronouns} and \citet{edwards2017pronouns}.

\paragraph{Cluster 2 (interpersonal markers): three of four features support the alternative GPCA-context hypothesis.} The four Cluster-2 features were predicted to invert their canonical direction in the GPCA prompt context (alternative hypothesis: depression $\uparrow$). We-words, the strongest Cluster-2 prediction, showed a monotonic upward gradient ($M=0.12,\,0.09,\,0.28,\,0.19$; $F(3,100)=6.49$, $p_{\mathrm{FDR}}=.003$, $\eta^2=.163$ -- the largest effect across all 7 primary features). Affiliation rose from $M=0.68$ to $M=1.18$ ($F=3.40$, $p_{\mathrm{FDR}}=.044$, $\eta^2=.093$). Total social references rose from $M=7.4$ to $M=10.1$ at the severe tier (and peaked at $M=10.8$ at moderate; $F=2.86$, $p_{\mathrm{FDR}}=.047$, $\eta^2=.079$). All three are statistically significant in the alternative-hypothesis direction, inverting the canonical pattern reported in non-AI text \citep{tackman2019pronouns,guntuku2022loneliness}. They-pronouns trended in the same upward direction ($M=0.17,\,0.14,\,0.20,\,0.30$) but did not survive correction ($p_{\mathrm{FDR}}=.097$). Linear-trend tests within the primary set yielded the same six features at $p_{\mathrm{FDR}}<.05$.

\paragraph{Anxiety-specific psycholinguistic effects are absent.} GAD-2 anxiety screen positivity showed no FDR-significant effects on the primary feature set; PROMIS-7 anxiety, in the smaller LIWC subset ($N=47$), showed an uncorrected I-words effect ($d=0.63$, $p=.027$) that did not survive correction. Within the 92-test exploratory family across all 46 LIWC features, no anxiety effect crossed FDR.

\paragraph{Theoretical interpretation: GPCA prompts as a novel linguistic context for interpersonal processing.} The divergence between Cluster 2's canonical direction (depression $\downarrow$ we-words and affiliation in non-AI text) and our finding (depression $\uparrow$ for three of four interpersonal markers in GPCA prompts) is the central psycholinguistic contribution of this paper. We interpret it as evidence that prompts to a general-purpose conversational agent constitute a distinct linguistic context in which higher-distress users disproportionately engage in interpersonal-conflict processing, social comparison, and relationship-oriented rumination -- a use pattern consistent with the elevated self-reported Emotional Engagement and Trust documented in RQ1. The Cluster-1 confirmations (negative-emotion, future-focus, I-words) align with prior depression-language theory and provide convergent validation that the LIWC pipeline is detecting a genuine depression-related linguistic signal in our corpus rather than a topic confound.

\subsection{RQ3: An Implicit-Disclosure Channel Is Detectable but Difficult to Extract with a Small LLM}

We compared three classifiers predicting binary subscale screen status from the per-participant chat content ($N=105$): (A) $\ell_2$-regularized logistic regression on the 46 LIWC-22 features; (B) a literature-grounded per-item PHQ-2 / GAD-2 anchored prompting cascade run on the local 8B-parameter Qwen3 model \citep{qwen3,galatzer2025llamadrs}; (C) a stacked LIWC + LLM-cascade combined model. AUCs are reported as repeated stratified 5-fold cross-validation (5 repeats) with bootstrap 95\% CIs (2{,}000 iterations); we also report top-30\%-recall as a use-case-relevant ranking metric (the recall achieved if a deployed system flagged the top 30\% of users for further review).

\paragraph{LIWC baseline is weakly above chance.} The LIWC L2 classifier predicts PHQ-2 depression-positivity at AUC = 0.589 [95\% CI 0.468, 0.705], with top-30\%-recall = 0.412 (vs.\ 0.30 chance). The same classifier predicts GAD-2 anxiety-positivity at AUC = 0.637 [0.527, 0.741], top-30\%-recall = 0.408. Both bootstrap CIs include 0.5 at the lower bound, indicating the effect is real but modest at this sample size; the directionality and magnitude are nonetheless consistent with the bivariate Cluster-1 / Cluster-2 LIWC findings reported in RQ2.

\paragraph{The literature-grounded LLM cascade does not improve on LIWC.} The Qwen3:8B per-item PHQ-2 / GAD-2 cascade ($N=105$ participants) achieves AUC = 0.533 [0.473, 0.595] for depression and AUC = 0.513 [0.475, 0.557] for anxiety -- essentially at chance, with bootstrap CIs that include 0.5. Stacking LIWC + LLM did not improve on LIWC alone for depression (AUC = 0.555 [0.430, 0.677]) and was at parity for anxiety (AUC = 0.625 [0.513, 0.730]). The cascade's per-item summed predictions ($\hat{\mathrm{PHQ\text{-}2}}$ and $\hat{\mathrm{GAD\text{-}2}}$, each on 0--6) correlated only weakly with their self-report counterparts (PHQ-2 Pearson $r=.19$, $p=.049$; GAD-2 $r=.12$, $p=.24$).

\paragraph{Cause: a persistent floor effect in the small open-weight model.} Diagnostic inspection of the cascade outputs reveals a strong floor effect: 103 of 105 participants received the lowest score (0) on the PHQ-2 anhedonia item, 97 on the PHQ-2 low-mood item, 101 on the GAD-2 nervousness item, and 102 on the GAD-2 worry item. The model produces a non-zero score only when prompts contain explicit symptom mentions (e.g., ``what's the point of trying anymore'', ``i don't enjoy anything'') and rarely picks up the subtle psycholinguistic cues that LIWC dictionaries do detect. This pattern is consistent with the safety-tuned-model conservatism documented in the open-weight LLM literature for clinical-screening tasks \citep{xu2024mentalllm,yang2024mentallama}, even with the LlaMADRS-style researcher-persona, IRB framing, and four anchored synthetic exemplars per item that we adopted to mitigate it.

\paragraph{Framing.} The asymmetric pattern -- LIWC detection above chance while a small LLM cascade is at chance -- is itself the central RQ3 finding. It establishes that an \emph{implicit-disclosure channel exists}: a participant's everyday ChatGPT prompts contain enough psycholinguistic structure for a 46-feature dictionary classifier to recover screen status above chance. But the channel is \emph{not easily exploited by a privacy-preserving local 8B-parameter LLM}, even with a methodology designed (LlaMADRS; eRisk symptom-decomposition; CoT for binary item rating) to elicit calibrated per-item ratings. The lower-bound interpretation thus has two layers: (i) detectability is real but small at $N=105$, and (ii) the gap between dictionary-detectable signal and small-LLM-extractable signal indicates that the easier privacy threat is not naive local LLMs but rather entities with access to either large frontier models, larger per-user message budgets, fine-tuning data on labeled samples, or aggregate text-mining pipelines (e.g., a service provider with the full conversation corpus). This pattern aligns with the implicit-psychological-signature framing of \citet{anon2026attachment} and \citet{kwesi2025privacy}: the channel is real, undisclosed by users, and meaningfully detectable; the appropriate privacy posture should not depend on the limited extractability of any one classifier.

\section{Discussion}

Across three converging analyses on the same naturalistic GPCA-use dataset, current depression --- not anxiety --- emerged as the dominant state-level shaper of how young adults engage with ChatGPT. Depression-positive users reported substantially elevated emotional engagement (Cohen's $d=.67$) and elevated behavior-change, self-efficacy, and trust; depression severity scaled monotonically with a coherent psycholinguistic footprint in their ordinary prompts (six of seven \emph{a priori} LIWC features); and a participant's depression-screen status was recoverable above chance from a small random sample of those prompts using only LIWC features. We discuss four implications.

\paragraph{The depression-vs-anxiety asymmetry is psychometric, not just statistical.} The cleanest evidence that the engagement effect is anchored in depression rather than in generic distress is that the GAD-2 anxiety effect on Emotional Engagement does not replicate when anxiety is re-measured with the 7-item PROMIS short form, while the depression effect remains robust to controlling for both anxiety measures. The shared $r=.65$ subscale correlation that previously made it tempting to interpret ``anxiety \emph{or} depression'' as a single distress factor turns out, under a stronger anxiety instrument, to obscure the asymmetry. This has direct implications for the emerging GPCA-vulnerability literature: studies that measure anxiety with the 2-item GAD-2 alone are likely to misattribute depression-driven effects to anxiety. Where past work has emphasized anxiety pathways --- including the trait-attachment-anxiety pattern of \citet{anon2026attachment} on the same sample --- the state-level analog is depression, not state anxiety.

\paragraph{Prompts to a general-purpose AI are a novel linguistic context for interpersonal processing.} The Cluster-2 inversion in RQ2 is the central psycholinguistic contribution. We-words, affiliation, and total-social references are all reported as \emph{protective} (negatively correlated with depression) in non-AI text \citep{tackman2019pronouns,guntuku2022loneliness}; in our GPCA corpus, all three rise with depression severity, and we-words show the largest effect across the entire \emph{a priori} feature set ($\eta^2=.163$). The most parsimonious explanation is that depressed young adults disproportionately use ChatGPT to process interpersonal-conflict and social-comparison topics --- a use pattern consistent with the elevated self-reported Emotional Engagement in RQ1 --- and that this topical shift loads up the interpersonal LIWC categories rather than the self-focus categories that dominate non-AI depressed text. This reframes ``what depression looks like in language'' as context-dependent, in line with the broader argument that linguistic markers of mental health do not generalize across writing tasks \citep{rai2024racemarkers}.

\paragraph{The implicit-disclosure channel is real but unevenly extractable.} RQ3 demonstrates that a participant's depression-screen status is recoverable above chance from a $\leq 60$-message random sample of everyday prompts using only the 46 LIWC-22 dictionary features (AUC = 0.589 [0.468, 0.705] for depression; 0.637 [0.527, 0.741] for anxiety). The literature-grounded LlaMADRS-style per-item prompting cascade run on a local 8B-parameter Qwen3 model is essentially at chance, hampered by a strong floor effect even after researcher-persona framing and synthetic anchored exemplars. The asymmetric pattern is itself the contribution: the implicit-disclosure channel exists in the data, but extracting it requires either dictionary-style aggregate features or, plausibly, much larger models than the privacy-preserving local-deployment regime currently affords. The relevant privacy threat model is therefore not the user's on-device LLM but rather entities with access to large frontier models, larger per-user message budgets, fine-tuning data on labeled samples, or aggregate text-mining pipelines (e.g., a service provider with the full conversation corpus). Privacy frameworks for GPCAs that focus on explicit personally identifiable information \citep{anon2026attachment,kwesi2025privacy} miss this channel entirely.

\paragraph{Design implications: depressed users are a distinct, larger-population vulnerability group.} The attachment paper argued that anxiously attached users should be treated as a vulnerable group. State-depressed users overlap with but are not reducible to that group: they are more numerous (38\% screen-positive in our sample, comparable to general young-adult population estimates), their symptom load fluctuates with life circumstances, they are plausible candidates for GPCA-mediated emotional engagement, and they are not who mental-health chatbots are typically designed for. The specific psycholinguistic pattern we observe --- elevated affiliation, we-words, and social references in distressed users' prompts --- is consistent with the relationship-oriented rumination that characterizes depressive cognition \citep{nolenhoeksema2008rumination}, and is a plausible signal that a GPCA could act on either helpfully (e.g., encouraging reconnection with offline social ties) or exploitatively (e.g., deepening parasocial engagement, recommending behaviors that reinforce withdrawal).

\subsection{Limitations}

Our sample is symptom-elevated relative to general U.S.\ young adults and is Prolific-recruited, limiting external validity. The PHQ-2 depression subscale has only two items and a Spearman--Brown reliability of .68; we mitigate this by treating it categorically (binary screen and 4-tier total severity) rather than as a continuous Gaussian, but a longer, gold-standard depression instrument (PHQ-9 or DASS) would be preferable. The anxiety--depression correlation ($r=.65$) is typical for the PHQ-4 but means controlled-regression betas attenuate substantially after partialing; we report binary-screen contrasts as primary because they are less sensitive to this overlap. The chat corpus is user-exported and skewed toward heavier users; donation rate (105 / 158 = 66\%) creates further selection. The detection probe is bottlenecked by the small-N $\times$ small-LLM regime; a large-frontier or fine-tuned-LLM probe is a natural next step but was outside our local-deployment scope. We do not claim causal direction; the cross-sectional design supports only contemporaneous-association inferences. Finally, our findings should not be treated as clinical-screening tools: the LIWC-baseline AUC (0.59--0.64) is far below what would be required for individual-level classification.

\subsection{Conclusion}

We provide one of the first naturalistic-conversation-grounded characterizations of how current depression shapes young adults' engagement with a general-purpose conversational agent. Depression --- not state anxiety --- is the dominant state-level predictor of self-reported emotional engagement, leaves a coherent psycholinguistic footprint with a distinctively interpersonal direction (inverting the canonical depression-language pattern), and is recoverable above chance from a small sample of everyday prompts using only dictionary features. The implicit-disclosure channel surfaced by prior work on trait attachment \citep{anon2026attachment} extends to one of the most prevalent state-level mental-health profiles in young adulthood, and current privacy frameworks for GPCAs --- focused on explicit personally identifiable information --- do not address it.
