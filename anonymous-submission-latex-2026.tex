\begin{abstract}
Prior work has shown that users' trait-level attachment styles predict how they relate to ChatGPT: anxiously attached users report higher emotional engagement, higher trust, and adopt more behavioral suggestions, while avoidantly attached users report lower trust and fewer self-efficacy gains. Attachment anxiety also leaves a detectable linguistic footprint in users' prompts, even in transactional exchanges \citep{anon2026attachment}. A natural follow-up question is whether this pattern generalizes to \emph{state-level} psychological vulnerability --- specifically, to symptoms of anxiety and depression that fluctuate within individuals and are routinely screened in primary care. We re-analyze the same participant sample ($N=158$ for self-report, $N=104$ for conversational corpus, $N_{\mathrm{msg}}=19{,}930$) and instruments, but swap the attachment predictor (ECR-SF) for mental-health predictors measured with the PHQ-4 anxiety and depression subscales, plus the PROMIS anxiety short-form as a robustness check \citep{kroenke2009phq4,pilkonis2011promis}. We ask: do anxiety and depression symptoms predict users' perceived ChatGPT experiences (emotional engagement, trust, self-efficacy, behavioral change, dependency concern), and do they predict linguistic patterns in users' messages, beyond demographics and usage frequency? Our sample is symptom-elevated: 49.5\% screen positive on the PHQ-4 anxiety subscale ($\geq$\,3) and 38.1\% on the PHQ-4 depression subscale ($\geq$\,3). Three findings stand out. First, \emph{depression, not anxiety}, is the dominant predictor of emotional engagement with ChatGPT: PHQ-4 depression correlates with the Emotional Engagement composite at $r=.27$ ($p_{\mathrm{FDR}}=.003$), and partial-beta in the controlled model is $\beta=.28$ ($p=.014$, $p_{\mathrm{FDR}}=.069$). Second, depression shows robust bivariate associations with seven LIWC-22 markers in users' ChatGPT prompts that survive Benjamini--Hochberg correction --- future-focused language ($r=.32$), we-words ($r=.30$), negative emotion ($r=.28$), total affect ($r=.28$), affiliation ($r=.27$), total social ($r=.27$), and they-pronouns ($r=.27$) --- while PHQ-4 anxiety shows no FDR-significant linguistic associations. Third, the linguistic signature of depression only partially overlaps with the attachment-anxiety signature identified in \citet{anon2026attachment}: both index affect and future focus, but the state-depression footprint is more socially oriented (affiliation, we-words, they-pronouns, total social) than the attachment-anxiety footprint (I-words). We discuss implications for privacy in general-purpose conversational agents, where current clinical symptoms may be implicitly disclosed through conversational style rather than explicit content.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

General-purpose conversational agents (GPCAs) are now routine in young adults' information environment: ChatGPT has over 700 million weekly active users, and nearly six in ten U.S.\ adults under 30 report regular use. Unlike traditional search engines, ChatGPT's anthropomorphic, multi-turn interface invites conversation rather than querying, and a growing body of work shows that users bring emotional and relational patterns to these interactions even in ostensibly transactional contexts \citep{brandtzaeg2017chatbots,chatterji2025chatgptuse}. Recent evidence has linked adult \emph{attachment style} to how users experience ChatGPT: attachment anxiety predicts heightened emotional engagement, trust, and uptake of AI-suggested behaviors, while attachment avoidance predicts the inverse pattern for trust and self-efficacy, with detectable linguistic correlates in users' prompts \citep{anon2026attachment}.

Attachment style is, however, a \emph{trait} construct --- stable across years and rooted in early caregiving history \citep{fraley2019attachment,bartholomew1991styles}. If the concern motivating the attachment literature is that vulnerable users disproportionately seek out, trust, and confide in GPCAs, then an equally urgent empirical question is whether \emph{state-level} mental-health symptoms --- the anxiety and depression that fluctuate with life circumstances, and that primary care routinely screens with instruments like the PHQ-4 \citep{kroenke2009phq4} --- show the same pattern. Anxiety and depression are substantially more prevalent than clinically elevated attachment insecurity: roughly 20--30\% of U.S.\ young adults meet threshold symptom criteria at any given time \citep{nimh2023yamh}, and this prevalence rose sharply during and after the COVID-19 period \citep{czeisler2020mh}. Any vulnerability mechanism that activates through conversational AI therefore has substantially larger population exposure when it operates through depression or anxiety symptoms than through trait attachment alone.

Prior work has examined mental-health \emph{outcomes} of AI chatbot use --- reduced anxiety from sustained companion-CA use \citep{maples2024loneliness}, reduced depression symptoms \citep{dosovitsky2021depression}, but also increased loneliness among heavy users \citep{phang2025affective}, emotional dependence \citep{laestadius2024replika}, and elevated risk in safety-critical contexts for distressed adolescents \citep{clark2025limits}. Much of this work treats mental health as the dependent variable: does AI use cause changes in symptoms? The question we ask is the converse: do current anxiety and depression symptoms shape \emph{how} users experience and engage with a GPCA that is not marketed as a mental-health tool? If the answer is yes --- and particularly if symptoms leave a linguistic trace in users' prompts in ways users would not notice themselves disclosing --- this has direct implications for privacy, design, and regulation of the commercial systems that now mediate a substantial share of adults' information-seeking \citep{euact2026,gdpr}.

In this paper we re-analyze the same $N=168$ sample of young-adult ChatGPT users (ages 18--25) and $N_{\mathrm{msg}}=19{,}930$ of their real conversational messages as \citet{anon2026attachment}, using identical instruments and analytic design but swapping the trait-attachment predictors (ECR-SF) for mental-health predictors. Specifically, we measure anxiety with the PHQ-4 anxiety subscale and depression with the PHQ-4 depression subscale, with the 7-item PROMIS anxiety short form as a robustness check \citep{pilkonis2011promis}. We predict five ChatGPT-experience composites (emotional engagement, trust, self-efficacy, behavioral change, dependency concern) from anxiety and depression in hierarchical regressions that control for age, gender, and ChatGPT usage frequency. In parallel, we correlate anxiety and depression with 46 LIWC-22 psycholinguistic markers \citep{boyd2022liwc} aggregated per participant across their ChatGPT messages, applying the same $\Delta R^2$ and Benjamini--Hochberg FDR correction used in the attachment analysis \citep{benjamini1995fdr}.

We ask three research questions:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{RQ1.} Do anxiety and depression predict users' perceived experiences with ChatGPT (emotional engagement, trust, self-efficacy, behavioral change, dependency concern), beyond demographics and usage frequency?
\item \textbf{RQ2.} Do anxiety and depression leave detectable psycholinguistic traces in users' ChatGPT prompts, beyond demographic and volume controls?
\item \textbf{RQ3.} Does the mental-health pattern converge with, extend, or diverge from the attachment pattern reported in \citet{anon2026attachment} on the same sample?
\end{itemize}

Our results point to three findings. First, \emph{depression, not anxiety}, is the dominant state-level predictor of perceived experience with ChatGPT: PHQ-4 depression is the only FDR-significant bivariate predictor of the Emotional Engagement composite, and its partial beta in the controlled model remains substantial. Second, PHQ-4 depression is associated with a coherent psycholinguistic pattern in users' ChatGPT prompts --- elevated affect, future focus, we- and they-pronouns, affiliation, and social references --- that survives FDR correction at the bivariate level. PHQ-4 anxiety shows no FDR-significant linguistic associations. Third, the convergent structure of attachment-anxiety and state-depression effects (both predict engagement and leave a linguistic footprint) suggests that the privacy risk raised in prior work --- users implicitly disclosing psychological vulnerability through how they talk, not only what they say --- extends to common clinical populations, not only to those with insecure trait attachment. Where the state-depression footprint differs from the trait-anxiety footprint is its distinctly social quality (affiliation, we-words, they-words), consistent with depression's well-documented links to rumination about relationships and social withdrawal.

We frame these findings as hypothesis-generating. Our sample is the same Prolific-recruited young-adult cohort used in the attachment study; it is not population-representative, and mental-health symptom rates in it are unusually high (49.5\% anxiety-positive, 38.1\% depression-positive on PHQ-4 screening thresholds). We report effect sizes, 95\% confidence intervals, and FDR-corrected $p$-values throughout, and we clearly flag which associations survive correction. Where we make design or policy claims, we align with prior calls to expand privacy frameworks beyond explicit PII to cover \emph{implicit psychological signatures} conveyed through conversational style \citep{anon2026attachment}.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Mental Health and Conversational AI}
A large body of work has examined AI chatbot use as a \emph{cause} of mental-health change: reduced loneliness and anxiety from sustained companion-CA use \citep{maples2024loneliness}, reduced depression symptoms from therapy-focused CAs \citep{dosovitsky2021depression}, but also increased loneliness among heavy users \citep{phang2025affective}, emotional dependence on companion platforms \citep{laestadius2024replika}, and safety failures in crisis-adjacent adolescent conversations \citep{clark2025limits}. Our question is the converse: does current symptom load shape how users approach and experience a non-therapeutic GPCA? This framing is operationally closer to the attachment work that directly motivated us \citep{anon2026attachment}, and closer to population-level questions about who is most exposed.

\subsection{The Trait--State Distinction}
Attachment style is a trait construct, stable over years and measured with the ECR-SF \citep{wei2007ecrsf,bartholomew1991styles}. Anxiety and depression as measured by the PHQ-4 are explicitly state-like, indexing symptoms over the past two weeks \citep{kroenke2009phq4}; the PROMIS short forms measure state over seven days \citep{pilkonis2011promis}. The two levels are correlated but distinct: insecurely attached individuals are at elevated risk for depression and anxiety, but current mood can fluctuate widely for any given trait profile. Because state symptoms are far more prevalent than clinically elevated trait attachment insecurity, they are the more population-relevant lens for thinking about GPCA-mediated vulnerability.

\subsection{Psycholinguistic Disclosure and Privacy}
LIWC-22 \citep{boyd2022liwc} and related psycholinguistic frameworks have documented robust associations between everyday word use and both trait and state psychological variables --- emotional states, personality, mental health, and life outcomes. \citet{anon2026attachment} used LIWC-22 to show that trait attachment anxiety leaves detectable traces in ChatGPT prompts (I-words, affect, future focus, inverse analytical thinking), and argued that current privacy frameworks, which focus on explicit PII, miss these implicit psychological signatures. Our analysis tests whether the same framework applies to state symptoms.

\section{Method}

We conducted a secondary analysis of the dataset reported in \citet{anon2026attachment}: a two-part Prolific survey and exported ChatGPT chatlogs from young adults (ages 18--25) collected November 2024--July 2025. The present analysis re-uses participants, materials, and corpus preparation; our divergence is in the predictor set (PHQ-4 anxiety + depression rather than ECR-SF anxiety + avoidance).

\subsection{Participants}
The parent dataset recruited 168 young adults who completed both parts of the survey. For the mental-health analysis presented here, we retain the same sample and filter by complete data on the predictors of interest. The regression analysis (RQ1) uses $N=158$ participants with complete PHQ-4, age, ChatGPT usage-frequency, and AI-experience data (gender missing for 38 participants, mean-imputed). The LIWC analysis (RQ2) uses the $N=104$ participants who additionally contributed chat logs that passed the parent study's quality filters (user messages under 20 words, English, $\geq 10$ conversations).

\subsection{Materials}
\paragraph{Mental-health measures.} We used the four-item PHQ-4 \citep{kroenke2009phq4}, scored as two separate two-item subscales: anxiety (``nervous, anxious, on edge''; ``not being able to stop or control worrying'') and depression (``feeling down, depressed, hopeless''; ``little interest or pleasure''). Each item is rated on a 4-point scale (0 = \emph{not at all}, 3 = \emph{nearly every day}); subscale totals range 0--6, with scores $\geq 3$ indicating a positive clinical screen. Two-item reliabilities (Spearman--Brown) were .80 (anxiety) and .68 (depression); the 4-item combined Cronbach's $\alpha$ was .83. As a robustness check we also computed the 7-item PROMIS anxiety short form \citep{pilkonis2011promis}.

\paragraph{ChatGPT experiences.} We used the 11-item ChatGPT-experiences instrument from the parent study (Table~2 of \citealp{anon2026attachment}), scored as three composites plus two single items: Emotional Engagement ($\alpha=.88$), Self-Efficacy ($\alpha=.71$), Behavioral Change ($\alpha=.71$), Trust (Q1), and Dependency Concern (Q13).

\paragraph{Covariates.} We controlled for age, gender (mean-imputed where missing, following the parent study), and self-reported ChatGPT usage frequency (ordinal, 0 = \emph{hadn't used} to 4 = \emph{20+ times in past two weeks}).

\subsection{Sample Characteristics}
Age $M=21.66$, $SD=2.23$; 49\% female (of those with sex reported). PHQ-4 anxiety: $M=2.59$, $SD=1.79$ (49.5\% $\geq 3$). PHQ-4 depression: $M=1.91$, $SD=1.57$ (38.1\% $\geq 3$). Combined PHQ-4 total: 35.4\% no distress (0--2), 37.3\% mild (3--5), 35.4\% moderate-to-severe (6--12). Anxiety and depression subscales correlated $r=.65$, as expected given their shared distress variance. See Figure~\ref{fig:dist} for distributions.

\subsection{Analytic Strategy}
We follow the analytic design of \citet{anon2026attachment} exactly. For RQ1, we first compute Pearson correlations between each PHQ-4 subscale and each AI-experience outcome, then estimate hierarchical multiple regressions per outcome: Step 1 includes age, gender, and usage frequency; Step 2 adds PHQ-4 anxiety and PHQ-4 depression. $\Delta R^2$ between steps indexes the incremental variance explained by mental health. For RQ2, we aggregate LIWC-22 psycholinguistic markers per participant across their ChatGPT user messages ($<20$ words, to exclude pasted essays and code), correlate per-participant averages with PHQ-4 anxiety and depression, and then refit controlled OLS models adjusting for age, gender, and log-message-volume. All $p$-values are Benjamini--Hochberg FDR-corrected within each predictor $\times$ outcome family \citep{benjamini1995fdr}. Variance Inflation Factors were computed on all Step-2 predictors; the maximum VIF across models was 1.67, indicating no multicollinearity concern despite the moderate anxiety--depression correlation. Residual normality was assessed per model with Shapiro--Wilk, Q--Q, and residuals-vs-fitted plots (all available in \texttt{results/}).

\section{Results}

\subsection{RQ1: Depression, Not Anxiety, Predicts Emotional Engagement with ChatGPT}

Figure~\ref{fig:reg} shows standardized betas with 95\% confidence intervals from the Step-2 hierarchical regressions ($N=158$), and Table~\ref{tab:reg} reports the full bivariate and controlled estimates. The attachment-anxiety pattern does \emph{not} replicate at the state level: PHQ-4 anxiety was not an FDR-significant predictor of any of the five AI-experience outcomes in either bivariate or controlled models. Its largest bivariate effect was on Trust ($r=.18$, $p=.020$, $p_{\mathrm{FDR}}=.102$), and its largest partial beta was on Dependency Concern ($\beta=.30$, $p=.016$, $p_{\mathrm{FDR}}=.080$), both failing to survive correction.

PHQ-4 depression, by contrast, was the clear state-level correlate of perceived emotional engagement with ChatGPT. Depression correlated with Emotional Engagement at $r=.27$ ($p<.001$, $p_{\mathrm{FDR}}=.003$), the only bivariate effect that survived FDR across all $5 \times 2$ tests. In the controlled model its partial beta was $\beta=.28$ ($t=2.49$, $p=.014$), attenuated to marginal after correction ($p_{\mathrm{FDR}}=.069$). The $\Delta R^2$ for adding both PHQ-4 predictors to Step 2 was largest for this outcome ($\Delta R^2 = .043$), though not FDR-significant. No outcome showed FDR-significant effects for trust, behavioral change, self-efficacy, or dependency concern under the controlled model.

\subsection{RQ2: Depression Has a Distinctive Linguistic Footprint in ChatGPT Prompts}

Using per-participant LIWC-22 averages from $N_{\mathrm{msg}}=19{,}930$ user messages under 20 words ($N=104$ participants), we tested bivariate associations between each PHQ-4 subscale and 46 LIWC-22 markers spanning pronouns, affect, cognition, drives, social references, perception, and needs (Figure~\ref{fig:liwc}).

\paragraph{PHQ-4 depression showed seven linguistic markers that survived FDR correction}, all positive: Future Focus ($r=.32$, $p_{\mathrm{FDR}}=.042$), we-words ($r=.30$), negative emotion ($r=.28$), total affect ($r=.28$), affiliation ($r=.27$), total social ($r=.27$), and they-pronouns ($r=.27$). An additional eight markers --- positive tone, prosocial, analytical thinking (negative), social behavior, want, you-words, anxiety-words, and discrepancy --- were significant at uncorrected $p<.05$.

\paragraph{PHQ-4 anxiety showed no FDR-significant linguistic correlates.} The strongest uncorrected associations were lack-words ($r=-.27$, $p=.005$) and negative emotion ($r=.24$, $p=.013$); both failed correction.

In the controlled regressions (adjusting for age, gender, log-message-volume, and the other PHQ-4 predictor), no LIWC marker survived FDR correction for either predictor, reflecting the harsh $46\,\mathrm{tests}\times 2\,\mathrm{predictors}$ correction and the shared variance between anxiety and depression. Uncorrected, the largest partial betas were for depression $\times$ they-words ($\beta=.40$, $p=.001$), depression $\times$ future focus ($\beta=.34$, $p=.006$), depression $\times$ total affect ($\beta=.32$, $p=.009$), and depression $\times$ we-words ($\beta=.30$, $p=.015$).

\subsection{RQ3: Convergence and Divergence with the Attachment Pattern}

Comparing our PHQ-4 results against the ECR-SF results reported by \citet{anon2026attachment} on the same participants yields three observations.

First, on \emph{perceived experience}, attachment anxiety was a broad-spectrum predictor in the parent study (emotional engagement $\beta=.43$, trust $\beta=.27$, behavioral change $\beta=.19$, self-efficacy $\beta=.13$, all FDR-significant), while PHQ-4 anxiety showed no FDR-significant effects. State-level anxiety is thus not a substitute for trait attachment anxiety: the behavioral-engagement pattern with ChatGPT appears to be anchored in enduring relational style, not in current worry.

Second, on \emph{emotional engagement specifically}, PHQ-4 depression plays the role that trait attachment anxiety played in the parent study. Both predictors converge on the same outcome (``I find it easier to share personal struggles with ChatGPT than with people''; ``I experience emotional relief after discussing personal matters with ChatGPT''), suggesting that engagement with ChatGPT as an emotional outlet is a final common pathway from multiple sources of vulnerability.

Third, on \emph{linguistic footprint}, the trait-anxiety and state-depression signatures partially overlap but are distinguishable. Both are associated with increased affect and future-focused language. Trait attachment anxiety was additionally marked by elevated self-referential language (I-words) and reduced analytical thinking \citep{anon2026attachment}. State depression is distinctively \emph{social}: we-words, they-words, affiliation, and total social references all rise with depression score, a pattern consistent with depressive rumination about interpersonal relationships rather than purely self-focused processing.

\section{Discussion}

Our central finding is that the vulnerability pattern identified by the attachment literature for ChatGPT --- users with relationally structured vulnerabilities engaging emotionally with a non-therapeutic system and leaving psycholinguistic traces --- also shows up at the state level, but it is carried by \emph{depression}, not anxiety. This has three implications.

\paragraph{Implicit disclosure extends to clinical symptoms.} The privacy argument in \citet{anon2026attachment} was that users implicitly disclose trait attachment through how they talk to ChatGPT, even in banal exchanges. Our results extend that argument to state depression: seven LIWC-22 markers survive FDR correction, tracking per-participant average language use across $19{,}930$ user messages. Because depression is substantially more prevalent than clinically elevated attachment insecurity, the population exposure of this implicit-disclosure pathway is correspondingly larger. Users with depressive symptoms talk measurably differently to ChatGPT --- more affectively, with more social and future-oriented language --- and the inference is available from prompts even when the user would not describe themselves as disclosing anything sensitive.

\paragraph{Design should treat the state-depressed user as a distinct risk profile.} The attachment paper argued that anxiously attached users should be treated as a vulnerable group. State-depressed users overlap with but are not reducible to that group. They are more numerous; their symptom load fluctuates; they are plausible candidates for GPCA-mediated emotional engagement and yet are not who mental-health chatbots are typically designed for. The specific pattern we observe --- elevated affiliation, we-words, and social references --- is consistent with the relationship-oriented rumination that characterizes depressive cognition \citep{nolenhoeksema2008rumination}, and is a plausible signal that a GPCA could act on either helpfully (e.g., encouraging reconnection with actual social ties) or exploitatively (e.g., deepening parasocial engagement).

\paragraph{Anxiety symptoms may not track engagement in the same way trait attachment anxiety does.} Our null result for PHQ-4 anxiety is substantive, not just underpowered: the predictor is reliable, the sample is the same one in which ECR anxiety showed $\beta=.43$ for emotional engagement, and the bivariate correlations trend in the expected direction but are dwarfed by the depression effects. State anxiety, as measured by PHQ-4, appears to be a noisier, less-engagement-predictive construct than trait attachment anxiety. This is a useful clarification for the emerging literature on chatbot vulnerability: the relevant trait-level anxiety dimension is interpersonal-relational, not general worry.

\subsection{Limitations}

Our sample is symptom-elevated relative to general U.S.\ young adults and is Prolific-recruited, limiting external validity. The PHQ-4 depression subscale has two items and an acceptable but not strong 2-item reliability (SB $=.68$). The anxiety--depression correlation ($r=.65$) is typical for this instrument but means the unique variance available to each predictor is modest, inflating the correction burden in controlled LIWC models; our finding that no LIWC marker survives FDR after partialing is consistent with this. The chat corpus is user-exported and skewed toward heavier users. The effect sizes we report are small to small-to-medium; we do not claim causal direction, and our findings are hypothesis-generating for future longitudinal and dyadic work.

\subsection{Conclusion}

Re-analyzing the \citet{anon2026attachment} dataset with PHQ-4 mental-health predictors in place of ECR-SF attachment predictors, we find that depression --- not anxiety --- is the state-level analog of the attachment-anxiety engagement pattern with ChatGPT. Depression predicts emotional engagement and leaves a distinctive, socially oriented linguistic footprint in users' ChatGPT prompts. Because depression is far more prevalent than clinically elevated trait attachment insecurity, the privacy risks surfaced by the attachment work --- implicit disclosure through linguistic style in transactional exchanges with general-purpose conversational agents --- apply to a substantially larger slice of GPCA users than the original analysis made visible.
